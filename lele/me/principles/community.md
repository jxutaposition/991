# Principles — Community

## Experts are ambitious professionals, not community servants

Programs fail when they assume members participate out of altruism. Experts are motivated by status, revenue progression, and social proof. Design for that — make tier progression visible, make recognition public, make benefits concrete.

*Evidence: Clay tier status drove partner wins in sales conversations. HeyReach premium partners do comarketing because it benefits them, not because they love HeyReach.*

## Selectivity preserves program value

Accepting everyone dilutes the signal. A selective program signals that being in it means something. Reject or waitlist the majority — not to be exclusionary, but because quality association compounds.

*Evidence: Clay rejected 80-90% of monthly applicants. HeyReach's 59 active experts out of a 70-person waitlist reflects the same instinct.*

## Tier progression creates career paths

Tiers aren't vanity — they create a roadmap. Members who can see the next level and what it takes to get there stay engaged longer. The path matters as much as the destination.

## In-person events drive high-trust, indirect lead generation

Hosts don't sell at events. The value is personal brand and network effects that compound into inbound clients. "The most level of recognition you can get from your local community." You can't measure this quarterly — it's a 12-month signal.

## Teams of 2-3 outperform solo operators

Single-person event hosts burn out and tend to sell (bad attendee experience). Teams with clear role delegation (Technical, Logistics, Personality) produce better events and sustain longer.

## Blanket budgets beat per-city pricing

Operationally simpler to give every host the same budget even if some cities are cheaper. Non-tech hubs require more effort to pull attendees — the flat rate compensates for that friction. Accept minor leakage to preserve simplicity.
