# Methodology — Discovery

Discovery is structured, not open-ended. The goal is to surface the five things that actually determine the shape of the work: goals, bottlenecks, tools, high-leverage channels, and success definition.

## The five discovery areas

1. **Goals** — What does success look like in 3 months? 12 months? What metric would make the CMO/CRO say "this is working"?
2. **Bottlenecks** — Where does the team spend manual time that shouldn't be manual? Where do things break or require human intervention every week?
3. **Tool stack** — What's already in place? What access is already granted? What's owned by one person who might leave?
4. **High-leverage channels** — What distribution surfaces are underused? Where are the compounding loops?
5. **Success metrics** — What are we tracking today? What should we be tracking that we aren't?

## The first questions to ask

- "What specifically were you hoping to get from this?" (forces clarity on the actual ask)
- "How much of this is already documented somewhere vs. lives in someone's head?"
- "What's working that we shouldn't break?" (scope the no-fly zone)
- "What's the one thing that, if fixed, would make everything else easier?"

## What to avoid in discovery

- "How are you feeling about the program?" — too open, produces vague answers
- Letting "we need help" stand without drilling into what help looks like
- Taking the first answer at face value on goals — the stated goal and the real goal are often different

## After discovery

Write a one-pager with: what you heard, what you think it means, what you're going to do, and what you need from the client to proceed. Post it in Slack. This forces alignment before a single hour is logged.
