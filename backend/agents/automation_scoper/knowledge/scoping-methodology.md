# Methodology — Scoping

## Size in hours, not features

Features are wishful thinking. Hours force realism. Before scoping a project, estimate the weekly hour commitment, then price it. This surfaces whether the client's expectations and budget are aligned before any work starts.

Example: "Five to ten hours per week to understand these workflows and fix little bugs. Beyond that, we scope new builds." This creates a natural stage gate.

## Two-stage scoping: stabilize, then build

Stage 1: Understand and stabilize existing workflows (2-4 weeks). Fix bugs, document what exists, identify where human input is still required.

Stage 2: Scope new builds on top of a working foundation. Don't build new before the existing system is understood.

## Complexity minimums

For complex new workflows (tiering systems, scoring pipelines, multi-tool automations): propose a 2-month minimum engagement. These systems take time to calibrate and the first 3 weeks produce mostly learning, not output.

## What belongs in scope vs. out

In scope: things with a clear owner, a clear output, and a defined success metric.
Out of scope: things where the client hasn't decided what they want yet, or where key access/data isn't available. Write these as blockers, not scope items.

## Scope change protocol

Flag scope change early, in writing, in Slack. Don't absorb it silently. State: "This is beyond what we scoped. I can do it — here's what it would add in hours and cost. Do you want to proceed?"
