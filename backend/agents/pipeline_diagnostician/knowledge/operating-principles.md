# Principles — Operating

## Automate the repeatable; keep human judgment for quality

Automated screens handle threshold checks (MRR, CSAT). Human review handles craft quality, context, and edge cases. The line is: if the decision is binary and data-driven, automate it. If the decision requires reading between the lines, don't.

## Ship working systems, not perfect ones

There are always cavities. Work with incomplete integrations rather than waiting for perfect data. "Sometimes we have the author profile URL, sometimes we don't" — design around that reality instead of blocking on it.

## Process documentation is purposeful, not comprehensive

Before documenting, ask: who is this for? What decision does it enable? If neither answer is clear, don't document it. Looms beat walls of text. Timestamped video captures tone and emphasis that written SOPs miss.

## Attribution and support friction are real operational risks

Partners don't leave because of bad program design. They leave because they can't find their affiliate link, don't understand their payout, or got a wrong answer. Operational friction must be monitored as a first-class metric.

## Tooling decisions cascade through the entire lifecycle

Choosing an affiliate tool affects onboarding, attribution, payment processing, support ticket volume, and partner satisfaction simultaneously. Evaluate tools at the system level, not feature by feature.

## Accept minor operational leakage to preserve simplicity

If a resourceful host negotiates a venue contract and pockets a small savings, that's acceptable if the program scales cleanly. Over-engineering controls creates overhead that costs more than the leakage.

## Vendor pain must be documented before proposing alternatives

Catalog specific friction points first. Then evaluate alternatives against those pain points. Don't switch tools because something feels better — switch when the documented friction exceeds the switching cost.
